For complex problems, you need to guide the model's reasoning process. These techniques enable the AI to solve multi-step problems that require logic and planning.

        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Chain of Thought (CoT) Prompting</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Chain of Thought is a breakthrough technique for improving an LLM's reasoning. Instead of asking for an answer directly, you ask the model to "think step-by-step." This forces the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps, which dramatically improves its accuracy on math, logic, and multi-step problems.</p>
            <blockquote className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-4">
                "When I was 3 years old, my partner was 3 times my age. Now, I am 20 years old. How old is my partner? Let's think step by step."
            </blockquote>
            <p>By talking through the problem, the model is less likely to make a simple calculation error.</p>

    <AccordionItem value="item-2">
            <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Self-Consistency</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>This technique builds on CoT. Instead of generating one chain of thought, you generate several by using a higher temperature. Then, you take the most common answer from all the different reasoning paths. This "majority vote" approach significantly boosts accuracy for complex reasoning tasks.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
        <AccordionItem value="item-3">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">ReAct (Reason and Act)</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>ReAct is a powerful model for building agents. It allows an LLM to combine reasoning with actions (tools). The model generates a thought about what it needs to do, then an action (like calling an API or searching the web), and then an observation based on the result. This Thought-Action-Observation loop allows the model to solve dynamic problems that require external information.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-4">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Multimodal Prompting: Beyond Text</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Modern LLMs can process more than just text. Models like GPT-4o, Gemini, and Claude can understand images, audio, video, and documents. This opens up entirely new categories of prompts.</p>
            <p><strong>Vision Prompting:</strong> Send an image along with a text prompt. You can ask the model to describe what it sees, extract text from a photo, analyze a chart, or identify errors in a UI screenshot.</p>
            <div className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-2"><p>"Here is a screenshot of my dashboard. What UI problems do you see? Suggest specific fixes for layout, spacing, and color contrast."</p></div>
            <p><strong>Document Analysis:</strong> Upload a PDF, spreadsheet, or slide deck and ask the model to summarize it, extract data, or answer questions about its contents.</p>
            <div className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-2"><p>"I have uploaded a 20-page research paper. Summarize the key findings in 5 bullet points and list the methodology used."</p></div>
            <p><strong>Audio and Video:</strong> Some models can transcribe audio, analyze video frames, or answer questions about media content. This is useful for meeting notes, podcast summaries, and content analysis.</p>
            <p>The principle is the same as text prompting: be specific about what you want the model to focus on in the image, document, or media file.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-5">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Practical Chain-of-Thought Examples</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Chain-of-Thought becomes most valuable when you apply it to real tasks. Here are concrete patterns:</p>
            <p><strong>For business analysis:</strong></p>
            <blockquote className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-4">
                "Our SaaS product has 10,000 users, 8% monthly churn, and $50 average revenue per user. We spend $120 to acquire each customer. Think step by step: Is this business sustainable? What is the customer lifetime value? At what churn rate does the unit economics break?"
            </blockquote>
            <p><strong>For debugging code:</strong></p>
            <blockquote className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-4">
                "This React component re-renders infinitely when I open the modal. Here is the code: [paste code]. Think step by step: trace the state changes that occur when the modal opens, identify the dependency that triggers the re-render loop, and suggest the fix."
            </blockquote>
            <p><strong>For writing:</strong></p>
            <blockquote className="border-l-4 border-primary/50 pl-4 italic my-4">
                "I need a LinkedIn post about remote work productivity. Before writing, think step by step: What is the counterintuitive angle? What data point would make people stop scrolling? What is the one actionable takeaway? Now write the post."
            </blockquote>
            <p>The key pattern: describe the problem, provide the relevant data, then explicitly ask the model to reason through it before generating output. This consistently produces higher quality results than a direct request.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-6">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Which Reasoning Strategy to Use?</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Each reasoning strategy fits a different type of problem. Here is a decision guide:</p>
            <p><strong>Use Chain of Thought when:</strong></p>
            <ul>
                <li>The problem requires multi-step math or logic</li>
                <li>You need to trace through a decision process</li>
                <li>The task involves comparing multiple options</li>
                <li>Example: calculating ROI, debugging code, analyzing data</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Use Self-Consistency when:</strong></p>
            <ul>
                <li>The answer must be factually correct (not creative)</li>
                <li>You can afford to generate multiple responses (higher cost)</li>
                <li>The task has one correct answer, not multiple valid answers</li>
                <li>Example: medical diagnosis reasoning, legal analysis, financial calculations</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Use ReAct when:</strong></p>
            <ul>
                <li>The task requires external information you do not have</li>
                <li>The model needs to search, retrieve, and then reason about results</li>
                <li>The answer depends on current or dynamic data</li>
                <li>Example: "What is the current stock price of NVIDIA and is it overvalued based on its P/E ratio?"</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Use Multimodal prompting when:</strong></p>
            <ul>
                <li>You have images, PDFs, screenshots, or audio to analyze</li>
                <li>Text alone cannot capture the information (UI bugs, chart interpretation, handwritten notes)</li>
                <li>You need to cross-reference visual and textual data</li>
            </ul>
            <p>Start with simple Chain of Thought for most tasks. Escalate to Self-Consistency when accuracy is critical and to ReAct when the model needs live data.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
