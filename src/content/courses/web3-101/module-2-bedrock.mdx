This module explains the core technology behind Web3. We'll break down what a blockchain is, how it works, and the important role cryptocurrencies play in making these decentralized networks function.

<Accordion type="single" collapsible className="w-full mt-4">
    <AccordionItem value="item-1">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">What is a Blockchain? (A Simple Analogy)</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            Imagine a shared, digital notebook that is copied and distributed to thousands of people around the world. This is a blockchain.

            * **Blocks are Pages:** Each page in the notebook is a "block" that contains a list of transactions. Once a page is full, it's added to the notebook.
            * **Chain is the Binding:** Each new page is cryptographically "chained" to the previous one, creating a chronological and unbreakable sequence. This cryptographic link makes it impossible to alter a past page without invalidating all the pages that come after it.
            * **Distributed and Synchronized:** Everyone in the network has a copy of the notebook. When a new page is added, everyone updates their copy. This massive redundancy is what makes it so secure. There's no single "master copy" to attack or corrupt.

            This creates a shared, immutable ledger, a single source of truth that isn't controlled by any one person or company. It allows people who don't know or trust each other to agree on a set of facts without needing a middleman.
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-2">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Consensus Mechanisms: How the Network Agrees</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            If there's no central authority, how do thousands of computers agree on which transactions are valid? This is solved by **consensus mechanisms**, the rules the network follows to reach agreement.

            * **Proof of Work (PoW):** The original mechanism used by Bitcoin. Computers called "miners" compete to solve complex math puzzles. The first one to find the solution gets to add the next block and earns a reward. This requires massive amounts of computational power and energy, which is both a security feature (it's expensive to attack) and a drawback (environmental concerns). Bitcoin still uses PoW.
            * **Proof of Stake (PoS):** A more energy-efficient alternative now used by Ethereum since "The Merge" in September 2022. Instead of competing with computing power, participants "stake" (lock up) their cryptocurrency as collateral. Validators are randomly selected to create new blocks, and if they act dishonestly, their staked crypto is "slashed" (confiscated). PoS uses ~99.95% less energy than PoW.
            * **Other Mechanisms:** The blockchain space is constantly innovating. Other consensus models include Delegated Proof of Stake (DPoS), Proof of Authority (PoA), and Proof of History (PoH, used by Solana), each making different trade-offs between speed, security, and decentralization.

            The choice of consensus mechanism fundamentally shapes a blockchain's characteristics: its speed, energy consumption, security guarantees, and degree of decentralization.
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-3">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">The Role of Cryptocurrencies (e.g., ETH)</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            Cryptocurrencies are not just speculative assets; they are essential for the functioning of a blockchain network. They serve two critical purposes:

            * **Paying for Computation ("Gas"):** Every action on a blockchain (sending money, minting an NFT, voting in a DAO) requires computational resources from the network. Cryptocurrencies (like Ether, or ETH, on the Ethereum blockchain) are used to pay the network participants ("validators" or "miners") for this computation. This fee is often called "gas." Gas prices fluctuate based on network demand, during high-traffic events like popular NFT drops, gas fees can spike dramatically.
            * **Economic Security (Incentives):** Cryptocurrencies are used to reward validators for honestly processing transactions and securing the network. Validators often have to "stake" a large amount of crypto as collateral. If they act dishonestly, they can lose their stake. This powerful economic incentive aligns their interests with the health of the network, removing the need for a central administrator.
            * **Governance:** Many blockchain protocols use their native token for governance. Token holders can vote on proposals that shape the future direction of the protocol, from technical upgrades to treasury allocation. This creates a more democratic system where the community has a direct say in how the network evolves.

            Crypto provides the economic layer that allows a decentralized network of strangers to coordinate and secure itself without a central authority.
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-4">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Wallets: Your Gateway to Web3</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            A **crypto wallet** is not like a physical wallet that stores money. Instead, it stores your cryptographic keys, the digital credentials that prove you own your assets on the blockchain.

            * **Public Key (Your Address):** Think of this like your email address. You can share it with anyone so they can send you crypto or verify your identity. It's derived from your private key.
            * **Private Key (Your Password):** This is the master secret that controls your wallet. Anyone who has your private key has full control of your assets. You must never share it. Unlike a password, if you lose it, there is no "forgot password" button, your funds are gone forever.
            * **Seed Phrase (Recovery Phrase):** When you create a wallet, you're given a 12 or 24-word phrase. This phrase can regenerate your private key. Write it down on paper and store it securely offline. Never screenshot it or store it digitally.

            Popular wallets include **MetaMask** (a browser extension), **Rainbow** (mobile-first), and **Ledger** (a hardware device for maximum security). Your wallet is your universal login for Web3, one wallet can interact with thousands of decentralized applications.
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
