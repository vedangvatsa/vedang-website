Blockchains like Ethereum can only process about 15-30 transactions per second. Visa processes about 65,000. This fundamental limitation, known as the scalability problem, is the biggest barrier to mainstream adoption. Layer 2 (L2) solutions are the answer.

<Accordion type="single" collapsible className="w-full mt-4">
    <AccordionItem value="item-1">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">The Scalability Trilemma</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Every blockchain faces a fundamental trade-off known as the <strong>Scalability Trilemma</strong>, first articulated by Ethereum founder Vitalik Buterin. A blockchain can optimize for at most two of the following three properties:</p>
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Security:</strong> The network is resistant to attacks and maintains the integrity of transactions.</li>
                <li><strong>Decentralization:</strong> The network is run by many independent participants, not controlled by a single entity.</li>
                <li><strong>Scalability:</strong> The network can process a high volume of transactions quickly and cheaply.</li>
            </ul>
            <p>Bitcoin and Ethereum chose security and decentralization, sacrificing scalability. This is why transactions can be slow and expensive during high demand. Layer 2 solutions attempt to break this trilemma by handling scalability on a separate layer while inheriting security from the main chain.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-2">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">How Layer 2s Work: Rollups Explained</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>The most successful L2 approach is <strong>rollups</strong>. The idea is simple: execute transactions on a faster, cheaper chain (the L2), then periodically "roll up" a batch of those transactions into a single proof that gets posted to the secure main chain (L1).</p>
            <p><strong>Two Types of Rollups:</strong></p>
            <div className="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-6 my-6">
                <Card>
                    <CardHeader>
                        <CardTitle className="text-base font-semibold">Optimistic Rollups</CardTitle>
                    </CardHeader>
                    <CardContent>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground"><strong>How:</strong> Assume all transactions are valid by default ("optimistic"). If anyone suspects fraud, they can submit a "fraud proof" during a challenge period (usually 7 days).</p>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground mt-2"><strong>Used by:</strong> Arbitrum, Optimism, Base</p>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground mt-2"><strong>Trade-off:</strong> Withdrawals from L2 to L1 take ~7 days due to the challenge period.</p>
                    </CardContent>
                </Card>
                <Card className="border-primary/50">
                    <CardHeader>
                        <CardTitle className="text-base font-semibold">ZK (Zero-Knowledge) Rollups</CardTitle>
                    </CardHeader>
                    <CardContent>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground"><strong>How:</strong> Generate a cryptographic "validity proof" that mathematically proves all transactions in the batch are correct. No challenge period needed.</p>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground mt-2"><strong>Used by:</strong> zkSync, StarkNet, Polygon zkEVM</p>
                        <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground mt-2"><strong>Trade-off:</strong> Generating ZK proofs is computationally expensive, but withdrawals are near-instant.</p>
                    </CardContent>
                </Card>
            </div>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-3">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">The Major Layer 2 Ecosystems</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>Several L2s have emerged as major ecosystems with significant user bases and application portfolios:</p>
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Arbitrum:</strong> The largest L2 by total value locked (TVL). Fully compatible with Ethereum, meaning any Ethereum dApp can be deployed to Arbitrum with minimal changes. Home to major DeFi protocols like GMX and Camelot.</li>
                <li><strong>Optimism (OP Stack):</strong> Known for its "OP Stack", an open-source framework that lets anyone launch their own L2. This has spawned the "Superchain" vision where multiple L2s share security and interoperability.</li>
                <li><strong>Base:</strong> Built by Coinbase using the OP Stack. Aimed at bringing the next billion users to Web3 with a focus on easy onboarding and mainstream consumer applications. Has the backing of Coinbase's 100+ million users.</li>
                <li><strong>zkSync:</strong> A ZK rollup focused on account abstraction and making crypto feel like a normal app. Supports native social login and gasless transactions.</li>
            </ul>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
    <AccordionItem value="item-4">
        <AccordionTrigger className="text-lg">Cross-Chain Bridges: Moving Assets Between Chains</AccordionTrigger>
        <AccordionContent className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-none text-base">
            <p>With multiple L1s and L2s, you need a way to move assets between them. This is the job of <strong>bridges</strong>.</p>
            <p><strong>How Bridges Work:</strong> You lock your assets in a smart contract on the source chain. The bridge then mints an equivalent "wrapped" version of those assets on the destination chain. When you bridge back, the wrapped tokens are burned and the original tokens are unlocked.</p>
            <p><strong>Bridge Risks:</strong> Bridges are one of the highest-risk components in Web3. They hold massive amounts of locked funds, making them prime targets for hackers. Over $2.5 billion has been stolen from bridge hacks historically. Some notable incidents:</p>
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Ronin Bridge (2022):</strong> $620 million stolen by North Korean hackers (Lazarus Group).</li>
                <li><strong>Wormhole (2022):</strong> $320 million stolen due to a smart contract vulnerability.</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Best Practices:</strong> Use native bridges operated by the L2 teams (like the official Arbitrum Bridge or Base Bridge) whenever possible. Avoid third-party bridges unless they have a strong security track record and multiple audits.</p>
        </AccordionContent>
    </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
